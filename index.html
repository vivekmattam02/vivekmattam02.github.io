Vortex: Real-Time Edge Perception for Autonomous Navigation via Foundation Model Distillation
NYU, Bell Labs Funded (MS Project), Jan 2025-Present

The problem. Foundation models like SAM2 and Depth Anything V2 deliver state-of-the-art perception, but they need server-class GPUs (300W, $15K+). Mobile robots run on edge hardware with hard constraints: 15W power, $250 budget, <10ms latency. The question is how much of that perception quality survives compression to a $250 Jetson, and is it enough to actually improve a robot's obstacle detection?

What I built. An autonomous 1/10-scale RC car (Traxxas Maxx 4S) that navigates hospital corridors using a distilled perception model running entirely on a Jetson Orin Nano 8GB. No cloud. No laptop. Everything onboard.

The distillation pipeline. I ran two large teacher models on an HPC cluster (NYU Torch, L40S GPUs): SAM2-Large (600M params) for semantic segmentation and Depth Anything V2-Large (300M+ params) for monocular depth. Their outputs supervised a single MobileNetV3-Small student (3M params) that predicts both depth and segmentation simultaneously from one RGB frame. The student is 300x smaller than its teachers and runs at ~5ms per frame on the Jetson via TensorRT FP16.

Why not just use a LiDAR? The car has a 2D LiDAR (RPLiDAR A2M8), and it handles localization via AMCL. But a 2D laser scan is a single horizontal slice -- it can't see chairs (beam passes under), glass doors (laser passes through), or overhanging obstacles (above the scan plane). The student model provides dense 3D depth and per-pixel semantic labels from the camera, catching everything the LiDAR misses.

Depth fusion. The Orbbec Femto Bolt camera provides hardware Time-of-Flight depth, but ToF has gaps on reflective surfaces, thin edges, and at range limits. The student model's learned depth fills those holes. The fusion node combines both: trust hardware ToF where confident, fall back to the student's prediction elsewhere. The fused depth feeds Nav2's 3D costmap for obstacle avoidance.

The full stack. I wrote 6 ROS 2 nodes from scratch: VESC FOC motor control with a custom UART protocol decoder (reverse-engineered for firmware 6.06), Teensy 4.1 encoder feedback and steering, TensorRT inference, depth fusion, class-aware costmap generation, and a Twist-to-TwistStamped bridge. The navigation stack uses Nav2 with an MPPI controller, EKF fusing wheel odometry + IMU, and SLAM Toolbox for mapping.

Hardware: Jetson Orin Nano 8GB, RPLiDAR A2M8, Orbbec Femto Bolt (RGB + ToF + 200Hz IMU), VESC MINI v6 MK5, Teensy 4.1, AS5600 encoders x2

Tech: ROS 2 Humble, Nav2, PyTorch, TensorRT, ONNX, Gazebo, Python, C++, SLURM, Singularity

Lunar Autonomy Challenge
NASA-style Lunar Autonomy Challenge, 2024-Present

Building an autonomous navigation system for a simulated lunar rover exploring unknown terrain without GPS or prior maps. The system combines ORB-SLAM3 for visual odometry with FoundationStereo for depth estimation in challenging lunar lighting.

Key insight: instead of treating depth as ground truth, we propagate uncertainty through the entire pipeline. The planner actively avoids shadowed craters and texture-poor surfaces where perception is unreliable. A* planning with perception-aware costmaps and pure pursuit control complete the autonomy loop.

Tech: Python, C++, ORB-SLAM3, FoundationStereo, OpenCV, ROS2, PyTorch

CityWalker-EarthRover Integration
NYU, Advisor: Prof. Chen Feng, 2024-Present

Deployed CityWalker (CVPR 2025) on a FrodoBots EarthRover for sidewalk navigation. The model was trained on YouTube walking videos and had never seen this robot, so the whole deployment is zero-shot. Built the full pipeline from camera input to differential drive motor commands: preprocessing, waypoint prediction, coordinate transforms, and trajectory tracking.

On top of this, working on Depth Barrier Regularization (DBR), a safety-aware training method that uses monocular depth estimation to penalize waypoint predictions that violate a safety margin. DBR converts depth maps into polar clearance vectors and adds a differentiable barrier loss during training, so the model learns to avoid obstacles without needing explicit collision labels. Offline results on CityWalk show a 15% reduction in depth violations and 23% increase in minimum depth margin while keeping navigation accuracy within 3% of baseline. Targeting CoRL 2026 submission.
